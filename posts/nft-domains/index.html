What are NFT-Domains and why should you care?

“NFTs are the future. But it can be scary to get started.”

NFTs have been all the rage recently within the crypto space, and while they do deserve their own article, for now, I’m going to assume you know the basics. That being that now files can be committed to the blockchain, making it nigh-on impossible to get rid of them, these files can be image files, text files, and now domain names.

With NFT domain names, it opens up the advent of people actually being able to own their domain names. Now before you send me an angry email saying “but I own my domain name, I have a permanent lease on it” I think it’s important to understand that traditional domain names cannot really be owned. It is impossible to own a traditional domain name, everyone (including the MAANG overlords) leases these domain names from the registry. You may pay a yearly fee for the right to use that domain name, but that is a far cry for outright ownership. NFT domains open up the advent for you to pay once, and permanently own your NFT domain, for example, I own the domain name ‘eddies-wallet.x’ (feel free to send some crypto to buy me a coffee some time). To make this possible, NFT domains are a product of a new web standard commonly referred to as Web3, an up and coming standard to enable a decentralized web (similar to NFT’s by themselves this 100% deserved its own article, so standby for that).

With traditional domain .com era domain names, the regulatory body that umbrellas these is ICANN. ICANN is a complicated beast to tackle in one statement but to keep this simple let’s just say they are the gatekeeper of the internet. They’re a non-profit that manages the domain name system to make sure they are all accessible, and when you type in eddiequinn.xyz it’s that site that loads and not another. This colossal system many of us take for granted when surfing the web is far more complex than many of us realise, and ICANN is constantly updating and managing to make sure this centralized system runs smoothly.

This system, while convenient, still has a major glaring problem. When you lease something the powers that be could one day just say “no you don’t get the right to use that anymore”. In a centralised system, someone always has the keys to your kingdom and it is seldom you. In this environment the benefits of blockchain shine, allowing for decentralisation inside this centralised ecosystem. Now as is always, blockchain deserves its own article but for now, we’ll just quickly address it. A blockchain is essentially a chain of records, stored on thousands of different machines across the world. The reason blockchain has been associated with crypto is because in its purest form it is essentially a ledger of transactions, open for anyone to see, which is stored across thousands of nodes, all constantly verifying both the database and each other. No one person is holding the keys, and this distributed ownership makes the scenario of losing your kingdom almost impossible to come into fruition. Income’s NFT domains, buy it once, own it forever. It piggybacks off this newly created blockchain eco-system, and now the keys to your kingdom are held by no one but you. No one can just remove your presence from the internet.

However, this new system has its own problems, as I mentioned before the centralised system is convenient. First off, you cannot buy a .com or .net domain name from this system, all the legacy domain names you’re used to are from that old centralized system, and the powers that be have a vested interest in keeping this subscription-based model in place. Web3 domains have to be unique, therefore, and thus far they are all pretty goofy

  • .zil
  • .crypto
  • .x

All of these names in their current state are like this for a reason however, they aim to act somewhat as a username for crypto-wallets. This system is what allows you, my dear reader, to buy me a coffee when you’re done reading this article, simply by typing eddies-wallet.x as the address. You no longer have to type in a massive random string. This system actually echos why domain names were originally made, because fundamentally every website is stored on a server, and it is far easier to remember google.com rather than the IP address of that server. This current use case, however, isn’t what these domains are limited to, 10 years from now when Web3 hopefully will be the standard system used, you will be able to host websites on them.

It is also worth mentioning that the two biggest blockchains holding these NFTs are Ethereum and Zilliqa. Out of the current ten domain extensions being sold via this method, 90% of them are on Ethereum, with the only one being held on the Zilliqa blockchain being ‘.zil’. This is not necessarily something unexpected, however, given that Ethereum is the second-largest blockchain with the largest being bitcoin.

Theoretically, you can use your NFT domain for something like a WordPress website, but not to the extent that would be ideal. The problem is right now NFT domain names are accessible out of the box mainstream browsers, right now only tech-head websites like Opera and Brave actually support them. You can load Web3 domains in google chrome, but it requires more modification than would be ideal. This means that the average user won’t be able to just pull out their iPhone, and type into safari eddies-wallet.x, and these sites aren’t even recognised by google.

Taking this rabbit hole server, the two building blocks for any website are the domain name and the WebHost. We now have a way to get the domain name decentralized, but this is basically useless if a ghost or AWS decided tomorrow to take you down. This is where decentralised web hosting comes in, via the ‘InterPlanetary File System’ or IPFS. IPFS works by storing pieces of your website across multiple nodes, meaning that there is no single point of failure. This system has redundancy built in to prevent any one person from being able to just remove your website files. This sounds great, but IPFS is still growing, they have quirks similar to Web3 domain names. At this moment in time, it can only really host static single page HTML sites, meaning no WordPress. Until this system picks up, the websites hosted with it will be similar to the 2000’s aesthetic.

This is by no means saying you should not invest in an NFT domain, unlike most NFT art projects they have legitimate utility. This utility is only going to grow in the future, and as this utility grows so will the price.
