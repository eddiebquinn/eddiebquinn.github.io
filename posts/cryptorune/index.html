Crypto-watch: Rune

Introduction

As time goes on, the crypto market is beginning to mimic the legacy financial system; this is especially true in the notion of lack of privacy. Everywhere you go, you need to provide KYC (know your customer), all the services you use require an intermediary, and everything you do is tracked to a T. This was not Satoshi’s vision when he gifted us the block-chain, and it is concerning to many of us.

This is not to say that no crypto projects are working to keep this industry free from big brother, the two big ones being ThorChain (RUNE) & Monero (XMR). Projects like these have a real potential to change the financial system forever

However, I did not want to leave you hanging, so to satisfy your blockchain knowledge wants, here is a deep dive on RUNE (my personal favourite crypto as of yet).

History & background

ThorChain was founded by a group of anonymous coders, at a Binance hackathon in 2018. The project technically has no founder and none of the 18 self-organized devs of the project has a formal title of any kind (you likely see how this appeals to me). Most are still anon, but a few have been revealed, namely Chad Barraford.

After raising $2m initial capital through private sales and seed sales in 2018 and 2019, and raising an additional $250,000 from its IEO on Binance. ThorChain’s first iteration (chaos net) went live in the summer of 2020. This was followed by the fully functional multi-chain chaos net in early 2020. It went on to become the back-end for the DEX BEP-Swap in September 2020

Up until this point, almost all the resources associated with the project (e.g. the website) have been developed by the ThorChain community. This would be concerning if it wasn’t for the absolute transparency of ThorChain’s core operations. Development updates are posted every week, and treasury reports every month, all of its code is open source. As of the time of writing has been audited 10 times by reputable names such as ‘Certic’ and ‘The Gauntlet’

What is it meant to be

Rune is meant to be a DEX protocol that allows for multiple cryptocurrencies to be exchanged across different blockchains. Unlike other solutions for this problem, ThorChain allows for this to be done natively and done w/out wrapping.

The other main contender in this space is UniSwap, which requires the factors discussed above. As well as this only lets you exchange cryptocurrencies on the same block-chain, and any across chain swaps require Wrapped assets

How it works

ThorChain works differently from this because it is an automated market. It uses a ratio of two cryptos inside a pool to determine the price. Cryptos are pooled with RUNE, the native coin of ThorChain. This is done with the intent to minimise the number of pools, which in turn

  • Maximises liquidity
  • Provides economic incentives to make ThorChain work

Thor was built using the cosmos SDK. Uses proof of stake consensus algorithm based on Tendermint. Currently, the network is run by 70 ± validator nodes which coordinate cross-chain transactions, but theoretically, it can support up to 360. ThorChain is intended to act as the BackEnd of a DEX interface, currently, there are multiple front-ends to ThorChain (mostly made by the ThorChain community)

Nodes Vaults
ThorChain nodes are responsible for witnessing transactions that occur in other block-chains. This process is mentioned below in the section labelled “the swapping process”. They also send and receive crypto from various wallets that they all have collective custody. Sending any crypto out of these collectively custodied wallets, however, requires the consensus of 2/3 of all active validator nodes ThorChain vaults store all the cryptocurrencies offered by the ThorChain protocol. This crypto comes from liquidity providers who pool an equivalent amount of Rune/Btc Rune/Eth or any other crypto offered by ThorChain.
ThorChain nodes must bond, or stake, 300,000 RUNE (£2.4m at the time of writing) to accept. In addition to this nodes must stake an amount of RUNE 1.4x the value of the cryptocurrency stored in its vaults. This is done so that if they attempt to steal the money in those vaults, the money they lose is greater than the money they gain via the process of slashing. On top of this ThorChain nodes are expected to attempt to stay anonymous; A large part of why delegating Rune is not allowed It should also be mentioned that ThorChain does allow asymmetrical deposits also. An asymmetrical deposit is where a user will despot unequal values of two assets in the pool. In their own words “a user deposits $2000 of BTC and $0 of RUNE into a BTC/RUNE pool. Under the hood, the member is given ownership of the pool that takes into account the slip created in price. The liquidity provider will end up with <$1000 in BTC and <$1000 in RUNE. The deeper the pool the closer to a total of $2000 the member will own
ThorChain Validator nodes are churned every three days for two reasons. To ehance the security of the protocol, and bring about any protocol upgrades ThorChain decides which cryptos to support using a pending pool. Investors can pool Rune and the crypto they want to see supported. The pool with the most assets is then added to ThorChain by nodes, making it trade-able by users

ThorChain validator nodes and liquidity providers earn a large cut of block-chain rewards and protocol fees in exchange for their service. To ensure the ThorChain remains secure, a mechanism called the incentive pendulum will increase/decrease rewards to nodes/liquidity providers to increase/decrease their staked rune

The swapping process & AMM mechanism

As mentioned before, all swaps facilitated with ThorChain trade against RUNE. The reason for this is that it is inefficient to create a pool for every possible cryptocurrency pair. To quote the ThorChain website “If ThorChain supports 1,000 chains, it would only need 1,000 pools. A competitor would need a whopping 499,500 pools”. This large number of pools dilutes liquidity, taking away from the trading experience.

Because all coins trade against RUNE, liquidity providers need to provide equal values of BTC and RUNE. As with other AMM style DEX protocols, arbitrage trades are incentivised to ensure this is the case. This is because changes would cause a discrepancy, and the arbitrage traders would jump on this. This would cause the price to go back to what it should be, meaning the process is self-regulating. This reliance on arbitrage traders is what allows ThorChain to remain decoupled from price oracles like a ChainLink.

Natively the reliance on trading against RUNE causes the issue of what if someone doesn’t want to trade against RUNE. This issue is however solved by front end DEX interfaces, which automate this process. In the background, the following is occurring (assuming a BTC/ETH trade):

  1. BTC is being sent to the BTC vault custodied by ThorChain validators
  2. The ThorChain validators pickup the transaction on the BTC blockchain
  3. The price of the BTC is determined by its pairing against RUNE, the same goes for ETH
  4. The nodes sent the appropriate amount of ETH to your stated ETH address

This whole swap process can take as little as 10 seconds, depending on the coins being sent and the whole process is paid for in RUNE. This rune then goes to paying network fees in the corresponding coins. In addition to this, it also goes toward a slip fee which is based on how much you’re changing the price, calculated using the ratio remaining in the pool. This slip fee is put in place to prevent whales

The tokenomics of Rune

The tokenomics of RUNE Rune began as a BEP2 token in 2019, with an initial max supply of 1bn. This was reduced to 500m at the end of 2019. Rune now exists natively on the ThorChain blockchain, but there is still plenty of Rune in circulation on BEP2 (Binance) and ERC20 (Ethereum).

Of runes total supply:

  • 30m sold to seed investors
  • 70m sold to pre-sale investors
  • 20m in an IDO, although 17m was subsequently burned
  • 105 million are held by the team to fund operations
  • The remaining 205m have been allocated for block rewards

The pre-mined block rewards for validators and liquidity providers have a ten-year vesting schedule.

Were it not for the generous team and private sale allocations, Rune would likely have the best tokenomics of any other crypto on the market. At any given time ThorChain validators must be staking rune worth 2x the total value locked by liquidity providers. The fact that DEX users need Rune to trade on ThorChain front ends, means that it has a similar economic profile to Eth (used to pay gas on Etherium)

It is likely the demand for ThorCHhain will continue to grow as it adds support for more blockchains and grows its ecosystem

  • Aesir Protocol - Forkless governance protocol for ThorChain ecosystem
  • Yggdrasil Protocol - Highly scaleable sharing solution for ThorChain
  • Asgardex - Fast and secure liquidity interface for ThorChain

Large amounts of Rune will also be required to bootstrap new assets to ThorChain. This is since nodes automatically begin accepting assets with the largest amounts of Rune pooled against them. In addition to this, ThorChain is also aiming to create

  • A decentralised stablecoin
  • A series of cross-chain DEFI protocols
